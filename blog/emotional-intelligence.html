What is EQ?

EQ ( Emotional Quotient or emotional intelligence) is defined as an individual’s ability to identify, assess, and control the emotions of oneself and of other groups. The term “emotional intelligence” was used in 1985 in a doctoral thesis “A Study of Emotion: Developing Emotional Intelligence” by Wayne Payne. It became popular in 1995 after the publication of Daniel Goleman’s best selling book “Emotional Intelligence: Why It Can Matter More Than IQ'' and it started a global movement to push EQ into practice in businesses, schools and communities around the world. The popular tests for EQ are Mayer-Salovey-Caruso Test (emotion-based problem-solving tasks) and Daniel Goleman Model Score ( based on emotional competencies).

What is IQ?

IQ (Intelligence Quotient) is a score derived from standardized tests that are designed to measure human intelligence. The first modern IQ test was developed in 1904 by Alfred Binet (1857-1991) and Theodore SImon (1873-1961) in French. IQ test is used to measure an individual's ability to learn, understand and apply information to skills, logical reasoning, word comprehension, math skills, abstract and spatial thinking, and filter irrelevant information. The popular tests for IQ are Stanford-Binet test, Wechsler and Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Cognitive Abilities.

Why is EQ Important?

Historically, IQ was believed to be the best measure for “success” in careers and life in general, but there are studies that show that there is a link EQ can also affect that measurement. Higher EQ can provide individuals with the ability to manage their stress and emotions, working and socializing in a team.
